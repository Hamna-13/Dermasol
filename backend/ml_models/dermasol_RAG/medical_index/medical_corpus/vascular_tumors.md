# Vascular Tumors

## What are Vascular tumors?
Vascular tumors are a subgroup of vascular anomalies characterized by true cellular proliferation (i.e., a growing mass of vascular cells). They are typically:

- Solid tumor masses (not fluid-filled spaces)
- Often progressive
- Usually not soft and not easily compressible

This helps distinguish them from vascular malformations, which are generally composed of abnormal vascular channels/cavities and are often soft/compressible.

Vascular tumors may be:
- Congenital (present at birth), e.g., congenital hemangioma, kaposiform hemangioendothelioma
- Acquired (develop later), arising in previously normal-appearing tissue

## Classification (high-level)
Based on ISSVA-style grouping, vascular tumors are commonly subdivided into:
- Benign
- Locally aggressive / borderline
- Malignant

### Examples listed in this section
- Congenital hemangioma
  - Rapidly involuting congenital hemangioma (RICH)
  - Non-involuting congenital hemangioma (NICH)
  - Partially involuting congenital hemangioma (PICH)
- Papillary intralymphatic angioendothelioma (rare)

## Infantile hemangioma
Infantile hemangiomas are the most common benign vascular tumor of infancy and represent the majority of benign vascular tumor cases.

### Key biology
- Driven by excess angiogenesis and endothelial cell proliferation
- Typically GLUT-1 positive on immunohistochemistry (supports diagnosis)

### Typical clinical course
- Often becomes noticeable weeks after birth
- Appears as a bright red “raspberry-like” spot/patch (especially superficial lesions)
- Has a characteristic timeline:
  - Rapid growth (first weeks to months)
  - Plateau phase
  - Involution/regression commonly beginning around ~1 year of age
- Many regress substantially over time, and many cases do not require treatment.

### Patterns and depth
Infantile hemangiomas may be:
- Focal, multifocal, or segmental
- Superficial, deep, or mixed
- Located in the skin (cutis) and/or subcutaneous tissue (subcutis)

### Superficial infantile hemangioma appearance
- Bright red, sharply demarcated, “berry-like” surface
- Often described colloquially as a “blood sponge”

## Congenital hemangioma
Congenital hemangiomas are rare benign vascular tumors that differ from infantile hemangiomas in several important ways.

### Key distinguishing points
- Fully developed at birth (present at delivery)
- Do not show the same postnatal proliferative growth pattern as infantile hemangiomas
- Typically GLUT-1 negative

### Subtypes
- RICH (rapidly involuting): regresses quickly after birth
- PICH (partially involuting): regresses partially
- NICH (non-involuting): does not regress

### Typical appearance
- Often bluish/livid rather than bright raspberry red
- May show a pale rim at the border
- Usually does not grow after birth (growth is not a typical feature postpartum)

## Other benign vascular tumors mentioned

### Papillary intralymphatic angioendothelioma
- A rare vascular tumor that involves lymphatic channels.
- Often presents as a slow-growing skin or soft tissue lesion.
- Diagnosis typically requires histopathology, and management is usually surgical when feasible.

### Pyogenic granuloma (granuloma pyogenicum)
- A common benign vascular lesion seen in children and adults.
- Typically presents as a rapidly growing, friable red papule/nodule that bleeds easily.
- Can occur at atypical sites (e.g., face/nose).
- Treatment often involves removal (e.g., curettage/excision with hemostasis), with recurrence possible.

## Diagnostic approach and when to biopsy
Some benign vascular tumors can look similar to other vascular lesions on initial inspection. When diagnosis is unclear, consider:

### Helpful clinical clues
- Was the lesion present at birth?
- Is there rapid expansive growth or infiltrative behavior?
- Is it non-compressible and forming a solid mass?

### Imaging
- Ultrasound can be useful, but may not always provide a definitive categorization.
- Imaging findings that are atypical or unclear should prompt specialist review.

### Biopsy (when needed)
If clinical exam + history + imaging cannot reliably determine behavior, a biopsy may be required to exclude malignancy, especially when:
- Behavior seems atypical
- Growth is aggressive or unclear
- Imaging is indeterminate
- The lesion is in a high-risk patient (e.g., immunosuppressed) or has unusual features

## Referral
If uncertainty persists, or if the lesion has concerning features, referral to a specialized interdisciplinary vascular anomalies center is often appropriate for:
- Diagnostic confirmation
- Management planning
- Coordinated care (dermatology, radiology, surgery, pathology)

## Notes on accompanying clinical cases/images (as described in the source)
Common scenarios highlighted in clinical references include:
- Infantile hemangioma in regions where growth can affect function (e.g., periocular involvement with visual risk)
- Congenital hemangioma showing rapid involution over months (RICH)
- Non-compressible vascular-appearing masses requiring biopsy to clarify diagnosis and exclude malignancy
- Pyogenic granuloma appearing in an atypical location and bleeding easily

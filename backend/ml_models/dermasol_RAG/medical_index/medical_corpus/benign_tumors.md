# Benign Tumors

## What are Benign Tumors?
Benign skin tumors are noncancerous skin growths and include hemangiomas, angiomas, angiokeratomas, seborrheic keratoses, dermatofibromas, lipomas, dermal cylindromas, acrochordons, epidermoid cysts, and sebaceous hyperplasia. Benign skin tumors are typically diagnosed based on the appearance of the lesion, but a biopsy may be performed to rule out malignancy if the diagnosis is uncertain or the lesion has concerning features, such as a rapid increase in size. Removal (e.g., surgical excision, cryosurgery, or electrosurgery) is typically only indicated for suspected malignancy, cosmetic reasons, or if symptoms are bothersome.

---

## General approach

### Diagnosis
- Usually clinical (appearance and distribution).
- Biopsy is considered when:
  - Diagnosis is uncertain
  - Lesion is atypical or changing
  - Rapid growth, ulceration, bleeding, pigment change, or other concerning features are present

### Indications for removal
- Suspected malignancy
- Cosmetic reasons
- Symptoms (pain, irritation, recurrent bleeding, recurrent infection, functional limitation)

# Vascular skin tumors

## Infantile hemangioma (strawberry hemangioma)

### Definition
A common benign vascular tumor of infancy caused by a localized proliferation of blood vessels.

### Epidemiology
- Most common benign vascular tumor in infants
- Often appears in the first weeks of life (may be absent or subtle at birth)

### Clinical features
- Superficial lesions: bright red, raised “strawberry” plaque/nodule
- Deep lesions: bluish, compressible subcutaneous swelling
- Mixed lesions: both superficial and deep components
- Common on the head and neck, but can occur anywhere

### Natural course
- Proliferative phase: rapid growth in early infancy (commonly first 3–5 months)
- Involution phase: gradual fading and flattening over years
- Some leave residual telangiectasia, fibrofatty tissue, or loose skin

### When to worry (high-risk locations/features)
- Periorbital (vision risk)
- Nose/lips (ulceration/scarring risk)
- Neck/airway region (breathing risk)
- Ulceration, bleeding, infection, significant pain
- Large, rapidly enlarging, or multiple lesions
- Functional impairment (feeding, hearing, movement)

### Diagnosis
- Usually clinical
- Ultrasound may help if lesion is deep/uncertain
- Further evaluation may be needed for segmental/large facial lesions or multiple lesions

### Treatment
- Many require observation only
- If treatment is needed:
  - Oral propranolol (first-line for problematic lesions)
  - Topical timolol (small superficial lesions)
  - Laser (selected cases, residual redness)
  - Surgery (selected cases, residual tissue/scarring)

## Cherry angioma

### Definition
A benign vascular lesion due to proliferation of dilated mature capillaries and postcapillary venules.

### Epidemiology
- Very common in adults
- Incidence increases with age

### Etiology/associations
- Often idiopathic
- Chemical exposure or hormonal factors have been proposed
- Can be associated with Fabry disease in specific contexts

### Clinical features
- Multiple dome-shaped papules or macules
- Typically on trunk and upper extremities (can occur anywhere)
- Size usually 1–5 mm
- Bright red to dark purple; thrombosed lesions may appear black
- Blanches with pressure; may bleed with trauma

### Diagnosis
- Typically clinical
- Excisional biopsy if malignancy is suspected
- Consider genetic testing if Fabry disease is suspected

### Differential diagnosis
- Pyogenic granuloma
- Petechiae
- Melanoma
- Blue nevus

### Treatment
Indicated for bleeding lesions or cosmetic reasons:
- Electrodessication
- Laser ablation
- Shave excision

### Prognosis
- No malignant potential
- Typically persists; no spontaneous regression

## Pyogenic granuloma

### Definition
A benign vascular tumor characterized by rapid growth and a tendency to bleed easily.

### Epidemiology
- Common in children
- Occurs in ~5% of pregnancies (often 2nd–3rd trimester)

### Etiology
- Unclear; may be associated with:
  - Minor trauma
  - Hormonal changes during pregnancy

### Clinical features
- Solitary polypoid or pedunculated nodule
- Can occur anywhere; common on mucous membranes, head/neck, trunk, hands/feet
- Rapid growth then stabilizes (often <2 cm)
- Colors vary (yellow to bright red/purple)
- Friable surface, scaly collarette; may ulcerate
- Bleeds easily after minor trauma

### Diagnosis
- Usually clinical
- Excisional biopsy if malignancy is suspected

### Differential diagnosis
- Amelanotic nodular melanoma
- Squamous cell carcinoma, basal cell carcinoma
- Angiosarcoma, Kaposi sarcoma
- Spitz nevus

### Treatment
Often removed because of bleeding and rapid growth:
- Laser ablation
- Shave excision with electrodesiccation
- Refer facial or recurrent lesions to dermatology

### Prognosis
- May regress in some cases
- Recurrence and satellite lesions can occur after removal

## Angiomatosis (including bacillary angiomatosis)

### Definition
Angiomatosis refers to vascular proliferations that may present as multiple angioma-like lesions. A clinically important infectious cause is bacillary angiomatosis.

### Key points (bacillary angiomatosis)
- Cause: Bartonella infection (classically in immunocompromised patients)
- Presentation: red-purple vascular papules/nodules that can resemble pyogenic granuloma and may bleed
- Distribution: skin and potentially systemic involvement
- Diagnosis: biopsy and histopathology (and additional tests depending on suspicion)
- Treatment: systemic antibiotics; specialist involvement is often appropriate

## Angiokeratoma

### Definition
A benign vascular ectasia of superficial dermal blood vessels with overlying hyperkeratosis of the epidermis.

### Pathophysiology
- May develop due to increased venous pressure (e.g., pregnancy, hemorrhoids)

### Etiology
- Often unknown
- May relate to genetic factors or local trauma

### Clinical features
General features may include:
- Intermittent bleeding
- Pruritus
- Pain

Features vary by subtype.

### Angiokeratoma subtypes

| Feature | Angiokeratoma of Fordyce | Solitary angiokeratoma | Angiokeratoma corporis diffusum |
|---|---|---|---|
| Morphology | Keratotic papules | Keratotic papule/plaque | Punctate lesions |
| Size | <4 mm | 2–10 mm | <1 mm |
| Color | Dark red to black | Dark purple to black | Dark red |
| Distribution | Multiple lesions on external genitalia | Solitary lesion on lower extremities | Numerous lesions on trunk/genitals/buttocks (“bathing-suit” distribution) |
| Other | Most common subtype | Common in men 20–50 | Associated with Fabry disease |

### Diagnosis
- Typically clinical
- Dermatoscopy: dark lacunae with a white veil
- Excisional biopsy if malignancy is suspected

### Differential diagnosis
- Other vascular lesions: cherry angioma, pyogenic granuloma
- Pigmented lesions: melanocytic nevi, Spitz nevi, melanoma

### Treatment
Indicated for suspected malignancy, cosmetic reasons, or bothersome symptoms:
- Surgical excision
- Electrosurgery
- Laser ablation
- Cryosurgery

### Prognosis
- No malignant potential

# Keratinocytic tumors

## Seborrheic keratosis

### Definition
A benign growth of immature keratinocytes.

### Epidemiology
- Very common in adults, especially >30 years

### Etiology/associations
- Usually sporadic
- Sudden eruption of numerous lesions may be paraneoplastic (Leser–Trélat sign), associated with underlying malignancy such as gastric adenocarcinoma

### Clinical features
- Single or multiple “stuck-on” soft papules/plaques
- Sites: trunk, upper extremities, face, scalp
- Texture: greasy, waxy, velvety, or warty
- May become irritated and bleed

### Diagnosis
- Typically clinical
- Dermatoscopy: horn cysts are characteristic/pathognomonic
- Excisional biopsy if melanoma is suspected
- Consider evaluation for underlying malignancy if Leser–Trélat sign is present

### Histopathology (general)
Variants exist; may show:
- Papillomatosis with monomorphic keratinocytes
- Keratin-filled horn cysts

### Differential diagnosis
- Macular lesions: solar lentigo, actinic keratosis
- Papular/plaque lesions: atypical nevus, melanoma, pigmented basal cell carcinoma

### Treatment
Indicated for suspected malignancy, cosmetic reasons, or bothersome symptoms:
- Cryosurgery
- Curettage
- Electrodesiccation
- Laser ablation
- Shave excision

# Fibrohistiocytic tumors

## Dermatofibroma

### Definition
A common small benign dermal nodule.

### Epidemiology
- Most common in ages 20–50

### Etiology
- Sometimes follows insect bite or trauma

### Clinical features
- Solitary or multiple firm papules/nodules
- Often on lower extremities but can occur anywhere
- Dimple sign: lateral compression causes central dimpling
- Slow-growing to ~3–10 mm, then stable for years
- Color varies: skin-colored, tan, pink, brown
- May itch or be tender; can bleed with trauma

### Diagnosis
- Typically clinical
- Excisional biopsy if malignancy is suspected

### Histopathology
- Fibroblast and histiocyte proliferation is typical

### Differential diagnosis
- Carcinoma including basal cell carcinoma
- Cellular dermatofibroma
- Papulonodular mucinosis
- Blue nevus
- Scar
- Pilar cyst

### Treatment
Indicated for suspected malignancy, cosmetic reasons, or bothersome symptoms:
- Cryosurgery
- Laser ablation
- Punch excision
- Surgical excision

### Prognosis
- Often persists for years; may regress spontaneously in some cases
- Local recurrence can occur after removal

# Adipocytic tumors

## Lipoma

### Definition
A common benign mesenchymal tumor of adipose tissue, usually subcutaneous.

### Epidemiology
- Common; can be solitary or multiple

### Associated disorders (rare)
- Familial multiple lipomatosis (autosomal dominant; multiple lipomas)
- Dercum disease

### Clinical features
- Soft, rubbery, mobile, discrete nodule/mass
- Typical sites: trunk, neck, extremities
- Slow-growing; commonly <6 cm but can be larger
- Usually asymptomatic; large lesions may cause compressive/obstructive symptoms

### Diagnosis
- Often clinical
- Ultrasound can help confirm
- Further evaluation (MRI preferred, or CT with and without IV contrast) if:
  - Mass >5 cm
  - Deep to fascia (firm/fixed or not freely mobile)
  - Rapid growth, pain, thigh location, or older age (concerning features)

### Referral
- If imaging is not consistent with lipoma, refer to a multidisciplinary sarcoma center
- Refer urgently if features suggest soft tissue sarcoma (e.g., >5 cm and/or increasing size, pain, deep location)

### Histology
- Normal mature adipocytes of mesenchymal origin

### Treatment
Indicated for suspected malignancy, cosmetic reasons, or bothersome symptoms:
- Minimal incision extraction
- Surgical excision
- Liposuction

### Prognosis
- Recurrence after excision is uncommon (<5%)

# Adnexal tumors

## Dermal cylindroma

### Definition
A rare benign skin appendage tumor.

### Clinical features
- Typically solitary tumor on scalp, face, or trunk
- Multiple tumors can occur in CYLD cutaneous syndrome

### CYLD cutaneous syndrome (familial cylindromatosis)
- Autosomal dominant
- Multiple coalescing tumors on head/neck (“turban tumor”)
- Tumors may include cylindromas, trichoepitheliomas, spiradenomas
- Often begins around puberty
- Malignant transformation can occur (reported up to ~10%)

### Diagnosis
- Consider genetic testing if CYLD cutaneous syndrome is suspected

### Treatment
Indicated for CYLD syndrome, cosmetic reasons, or bothersome symptoms:
- Surgical excision
- Scalp excision with skin grafting (selected extensive cases)
- Other options: curettage, cryosurgery, electrosurgery, dermabrasion

# Benign papillomatous lesions

## Acrochordon (skin tag)

### Definition
A small papillomatous lesion, sometimes slightly discolored, commonly arising in skin folds.

### Epidemiology
- Common in adults >50 years

### Etiology/associations
- Often due to chronic friction/irritation
- Associated with:
  - Hormonal changes (pregnancy, acromegaly)
  - Metabolic syndrome (obesity, type 2 diabetes mellitus)
- Perianal acrochordons can be associated with Crohn disease

### Clinical features
- Smooth round/oval pedunculated papule on a narrow stalk
- Common sites: lower neck, axillae, inframammary folds, inguinal regions

### Diagnosis
- Typically clinical
- Excisional biopsy if malignancy is suspected

### Differential diagnosis
- Senescent intradermal nevus, pedunculated nevus
- Basal cell carcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma

### Treatment
Indicated for cosmetic reasons or bothersome symptoms:
- Snip or shave excision
- Cryosurgery
- Electrodesiccation
- Refer eyelid lesions to dermatology/plastic surgery

# Cysts

## Epidermoid cyst (epidermal inclusion cyst, keratin cyst)

### Definition
An encapsulated lesion containing keratin and lipids, lined with stratified squamous epithelium.

### Pathogenesis
- Implantation of epithelium within the dermis

### Clinical features
- Dome-shaped firm mobile dermal/subcutaneous nodule or mass
- Often solitary; sites include face, head, neck, trunk, genitals
- Grows slowly over years or remains stable (about 0.5–5 cm)
- Skin-colored with central black punctum; erythematous when inflamed
- Usually painless; painful if ruptured, may become infected
- Contents: thick cream-colored keratinaceous material with unpleasant odor

### Diagnosis
- Typically clinical
- Incision and drainage if infection is suspected
- Excisional biopsy if malignancy is suspected

### Differential diagnosis
- Other cysts: trichilemmal (pilar) cyst, sebaceous cyst

### Treatment
- Asymptomatic: no treatment required
- Face lesions: prefer dermatology/plastic surgery referral
- Ruptured/inflamed:
  - May resolve but often recur
  - Intralesional corticosteroid (e.g., triamcinolone) may speed resolution
- Infected:
  - Antibiotics plus incision and drainage as indicated
- Definitive: surgical excision (ideally after inflammation/infection settles) to prevent recurrence

### Additional note
- Multiple or atypically located cysts (fingers, toes, legs) warrant evaluation for Gardner syndrome

---

## Trichilemmal cyst (pilar cyst)

### Definition
A cyst arising from the outer root sheath of the hair follicle.

### Clinical features
- Dome-shaped firm mobile subcutaneous nodule/mass
- Often multiple
- Most commonly on the scalp
- Contents: dense keratinaceous material

### Diagnosis
- Typically clinical
- Excisional biopsy if malignancy is suspected

### Differential diagnosis
- Epidermoid cyst, other cysts
- Nodular basal cell carcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma

### Treatment
- Surgical excision

# Sebaceous gland disorders

## Sebaceous hyperplasia

### Definition
A benign common disorder where sebocytes enlarge, producing small sebaceous gland overgrowths.

### Epidemiology
- Common in adults >30 years
- Can occur in immunosuppressed patients (e.g., on cyclosporine)

### Clinical features
- Solitary or multiple soft dome-shaped papules with central umbilication
- Typically on the face near hair follicles (forehead, cheeks, chin)
- Lateral compression may express sebum from the umbilication

### Diagnosis
- Typically clinical
- Dermatoscopy: white-to-yellow globules with peripheral superficial vessels (“cumulus sign”)
- Excisional biopsy if malignancy is suspected

### Treatment
Indicated for cosmetic reasons or bothersome symptoms:
- Topical bichloroacetic acid (selected cases)
- Lesion removal: electrosurgery, cryotherapy, phototherapy, laser ablation, shave excision
- Systemic isotretinoin for diffuse lesions (selected cases)

### Complication note
- Deeper removal (e.g., curettage) may cause pitted scarring
